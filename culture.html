The 1939 Film and the Legacy of The Wizard of Oz

Although The Wonderful Wizard of Oz was first published by L. Frank Baum in 1900, many people today know the story through the famous 1939 film adaptation, The Wizard of Oz. Starring Judy Garland as Dorothy, the film became one of the most successful and influential movies ever made. It was famous for its use of colour, particularly the moment when Dorothy leaves the black-and-white world of Kansas and enters the colourful Land of Oz. This visual contrast helped audiences experience the wonder of Oz in a powerful new way.

The film introduced many memorable elements that remain well known today, including Dorothy's ruby slippers, the song "Over the Rainbow," and the phrase, "There's no place like home." While the movie follows the main events of Baum's story, it also makes several changes. In the original novel, Dorothy's shoes are silver rather than ruby, and some characters and adventures are different.

More than eighty years after its release, The Wizard of Oz continues to influence popular culture. References to the Yellow Brick Road, the Emerald City, flying monkeys, and the Wicked Witch can be found in movies, television shows, books, advertisements, and video games. People often use the expression "pay no attention to the man behind the curtain" when discussing leaders or organisations that appear powerful but are actually hiding the truth. Likewise, describing someone's journey as "following the Yellow Brick Road" has become a way of talking about a path towards a goal.

The story's themes of friendship, courage, intelligence, kindness, and belonging continue to resonate with audiences around the world. As a result, The Wizard of Oz remains one of the most recognised and referenced stories in modern culture, inspiring new adaptations and retellings for each generation.
